|---|---|

| Timing | Morning, mixed into coffee |
| Reason | Muscle strength, performance, recovery |
| Brand | Not specified |
| Started | Not recorded |
| Evidence rating | **Strong** |
| Notes | One of the most researched supplements in existence. Well-established benefits for strength, power output, and lean mass. ### Whey Protein (MyProtein)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Dose | 42g |
| Timing | ~9am, post-workout |
| Reason | Muscle protein synthesis, recovery |
| Brand | MyProtein |
| Started | Not recorded |
| Evidence rating | **Strong** |
| Notes | Whey is the gold standard for post-workout protein — fast-digesting, high leucine content, well-studied. 42g is a solid dose post-training. Total daily protein intake is the more important variable for muscle retention and fat loss — aim for ~1.6–2.2g per kg of bodyweight (137–188g/day at 85.5kg). Current estimated intake is ~137–152g/day (shake + lunch + dinner) — sitting at the low end of the target range. Pushing toward the upper end (~185g) on training days would support recomposition better. |

### Vitamin D3 + K2 (MK-7)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Dose | 2,000–4,000 IU D3 + 100 mcg K2 (MK-7) |
| Timing | Morning, with food |
| Reason | Vitamin D deficiency is near-universal in UK adults with desk jobs; supports metabolic health, insulin sensitivity, immune function, testosterone, cardiovascular health, and longevity |
| Brand | Not yet specified |
| Started | Apr 2026 |
| Evidence rating | **Strong (D3) / Moderate (K2 pairing)** |
| Notes | UK latitude provides essentially zero UV-driven D3 synthesis October–April. Desk job means minimal outdoor exposure year-round. Target blood level: 50–80 ng/mL (125–200 nmol/L). Start at 2,000 IU; test 25-OH vitamin D after 2–3 months and adjust dose accordingly. K2 (MK-7 form specifically) helps direct calcium to bones rather than arterial walls — important when supplementing D3 long-term. Most multivitamins contain K1, not K2, and underdose D3 significantly. |

### Magnesium Glycinate

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Dose | 300–400 mg |
| Timing | 30–60 minutes before bed |
| Reason | Sleep quality, muscle recovery, insulin sensitivity, stress resilience |
| Brand | Not yet specified |
| Started | Apr 2026 |
| Evidence rating | **Moderate-to-Strong** |
| Notes | Magnesium is the second most common nutrient deficiency in Western diets and is further depleted by regular exercise. Glycinate form has good bioavailability and does not cause the laxative effect of magnesium oxide (found in most multivitamins). RCT evidence supports improved sleep latency and quality. Also involved in 300+ enzymatic processes including glucose metabolism — relevant to metabolic health and insulin sensitivity goals. 30-day avg sleep (7.2 hrs) is below 90-day avg (7.5 hrs) — this is a reasonable intervention to trial. Re-assess HRV and subjective sleep after 4–6 weeks. |

### Omega-3 (Fish Oil / EPA+DHA)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Dose | 2–3g combined EPA+DHA (check label — not total fish oil weight) |
| Timing | With dinner (fat in meal improves absorption) |
| Reason | Cardiovascular health, brain health, anti-inflammatory, longevity |
| Brand | Not yet specified |
| Started | Apr 2026 |
| Evidence rating | **Strong** |
| Notes | One of the most evidence-backed supplements for long-term health. High omega-3 index is independently associated with reduced all-cause and cardiovascular mortality. Anti-inflammatory effect is relevant given training load and excess body fat (adipose tissue is pro-inflammatory). Western diets are typically high in omega-6 and low in omega-3 — supplementation meaningfully shifts this ratio. Dose to EPA+DHA specifically: a "1,000 mg fish oil" capsule often contains only ~300 mg EPA+DHA, so check the label. Aim for 2–3g EPA+DHA total. |

## Evidence Ratings

- **Strong** — Multiple large RCTs support this use case
- **Moderate** — Some RCT evidence, consistent observational data
- **Preliminary** — Early studies, animal data, or mechanistic reasoning only
- **Weak / Unclear** — Little to no quality evidence for this specific use
- **Potentially harmful** — Evidence of risk at this dose or interaction concern

---

## Interaction Notes

- No known interactions between creatine and whey protein.
- No known interactions between D3+K2, magnesium glycinate, and omega-3.
- All five current supplements are safe to use together.
- Magnesium may slightly improve creatine uptake into muscle cells — no concern, potentially beneficial.
